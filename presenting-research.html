Amphibian Disease

Honors Thesis | Spring 2020

Of the 30-some Seniors in my department, only 6 of us chose to complete an honors thesis. I had recently taken a course on global change and emerging infectious disease, which opened my eyes to the fascinating and interconnected nature of complex disease systems. While the class was focused on human illness, I was particularly curious to explore how these interactions mapped onto a zoonotic (animal) disease, so I made this the focus of my independent research thesis

This was my first experience independently designing and conducting a research project and manuscript with little structure or guidance, so the entire process was a tremendous learning experience. The literature review portion was particularly valuable, as I was forced to take in a variety of information from past studies, much of which seemed to be in conflict, and use context to build a cohesive narrative. I also became versed in the formal paper style, and learned to describe and present observations without overstepping my confidence and â€˜explainingâ€™. Since my audience was academic but non specialist, I chose to convey information in a complex, yet generally accessible manner.

Being able to pick up on trends and connections, and convey them through writing is one of the most useful skills Iâ€™ve developed, and especially so within a formal scientific context. Looking back, my largest regret is not incorporating more of the human context. I believe science should be useful, and while my thesis was an immensely valuable experience, it reinforced my commitment that my future work be grounded in tangible applications.

Soil Science

Poster | Spring 2019

Science of Soils is one of several courses at Stanford that include a community-engaged learning component. My teammates and I put to use our knowledge and skills at soil chemistry by supporting a local home for alter-abled adults in selecting crops for their therapy garden. I created this poster for our final presentation to the class (another teammate designed a separate, more accessible document specifically for our community partners).

Because soil chemistry is dense and difficult to digest, even for students in the class, I used imagery to organize and enhance the significance of the text. By considering the context of each element of the poster, and arranging the components in an intuitive way, I was able to better communicate our work.

This was one of my first attempts at designing a scientific (albeit less professional) poster. My design skills have improved quite a bit since then, but the principles of context-based organization and graphical presentation remain central. Coral Reef Herbivory

Presentation | Autumn 2018

During my study abroad in Australia, I traveled from biome to biome engaging in a variety of place-based coursework and field research. Each student conducted a targeted research project, which we formally presented to each other at the end of the term. As you can see here, mine was a lab experiment predicting the impacts of increased temperature and light on sea hare grazing

This was my first experience with the full scientific process: designing a question, conducting an experiment, analyzing the results, publishing in a peer-reviewed journal, and finally presenting it. When constructing this presentation, I put my research in context, and considered the knowledge and interests of my audience when describing my work, drawing connections, and even just organizing and curating my content.

Since I intend to conduct marine research throughout my career, this was excellent practice at presenting my research to a technical audience in a formal setting. I have since drawn off these context-based slide skills when presenting my research in class, professional settings, and even an international conference.
